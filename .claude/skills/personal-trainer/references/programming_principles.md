# Training Programming Principles

Reference guide for exercise programming decisions. Apply these principles when generating or revising training plans.

## Progressive Overload

The primary driver of adaptation. Increase training stimulus over time through:

- **Load** — add weight (primary method for strength goals)
- **Volume** — add sets or reps (primary method for hypertrophy)
- **Density** — reduce rest periods (useful for conditioning/fat loss)
- **Frequency** — add training days (when recovery allows)

### Rate of Progression by Experience
- **Beginner** (< 1 year): increase load 2-5% per week (linear progression)
- **Intermediate** (1-3 years): increase load every 1-2 weeks, use weekly undulation
- **Advanced** (3+ years): monthly progression cycles, block periodization

## Periodization Models

### Linear Periodization
- Gradually increase intensity while decreasing volume over weeks/months
- Best for: beginners, strength peaking, simple programs
- Example: Week 1-4 → 3x12, Week 5-8 → 4x8, Week 9-12 → 5x5

### Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP)
- Vary rep ranges within the week (e.g., heavy Monday, moderate Wednesday, light Friday)
- Best for: intermediates, hypertrophy + strength, keeping training varied
- Example: Mon 4x5 heavy, Wed 3x10 moderate, Fri 3x15 light

### Block Periodization
- Dedicate 3-4 week blocks to specific qualities (accumulation → transmutation → realization)
- Best for: advanced trainees, sport-specific preparation

### General Recommendation
- Beginners: linear progression (simple, effective, builds habits)
- Intermediates: DUP or simple linear with deload weeks
- Advanced: block or conjugate methods

## Exercise Selection

### Movement Patterns (every program should include all of these weekly)
- **Horizontal Push**: bench press, push-ups, dumbbell press
- **Horizontal Pull**: barbell row, cable row, dumbbell row
- **Vertical Push**: overhead press, landmine press, pike push-ups
- **Vertical Pull**: pull-ups, chin-ups, lat pulldown
- **Hip Hinge**: deadlift, Romanian deadlift, hip thrust, kettlebell swing
- **Squat**: back squat, front squat, goblet squat, leg press
- **Carry/Core**: farmer's walks, planks, pallof press, hanging leg raise
- **Single-Leg**: lunges, Bulgarian split squats, step-ups

### Selection Priorities
1. Compound movements first (multi-joint, most bang for buck)
2. Isolation movements to address weak points or goals
3. Match exercises to available equipment
4. Avoid movements that aggravate injuries (always provide alternatives)
5. Include exercises the client enjoys when possible (adherence matters)

## Volume and Intensity Guidelines

### Sets Per Muscle Group Per Week
| Goal | Beginner | Intermediate | Advanced |
|------|----------|--------------|----------|
| Maintenance | 6-8 | 8-10 | 10-12 |
| Hypertrophy | 10-14 | 14-18 | 18-22 |
| Strength | 8-12 | 10-15 | 12-18 |

### Rep Ranges by Goal
- **Strength**: 1-5 reps, 80-95% 1RM, RPE 7-9
- **Hypertrophy**: 6-12 reps, 65-80% 1RM, RPE 7-9
- **Muscular Endurance**: 12-20+ reps, 50-65% 1RM, RPE 6-8
- **Power**: 1-5 reps, 50-70% 1RM, explosive intent

### Rest Periods
- **Strength (heavy compounds)**: 2-5 minutes
- **Hypertrophy**: 60-120 seconds
- **Endurance/Conditioning**: 30-60 seconds
- **Supersets**: minimal rest between exercises, full rest between rounds

### RPE / RIR Scale
- RPE 10 / 0 RIR = failure
- RPE 9 / 1 RIR = could do 1 more
- RPE 8 / 2 RIR = could do 2 more (good default training intensity)
- RPE 7 / 3 RIR = warm-up sets, deload work, beginners

## Age-Related Considerations

### Under 30
- Full recovery capacity, can handle higher volume and frequency
- Focus on building movement quality and training habits

### 30-45
- Recovery starts to slow; ensure adequate rest days
- Prioritize joint health — include mobility work
- May need slightly longer warm-ups

### 45-60
- Recovery noticeably slower; program deload weeks every 3-4 weeks
- Emphasize compound movements but with controlled tempo
- Include balance and stability work
- Be conservative with loading progression

### Over 60
- Prioritize functional movement, balance, bone density
- Lower intensity, higher frequency often works better
- Longer warm-ups (10-15 min) are essential
- Include flexibility and mobility every session
- Focus on movements of daily living (squat to chair, carries, steps)

## Goal-Specific Programming

### Strength
- Primary: 3-5 reps on main lifts, 80-90% intensity
- 3-5 training days/week
- Focus on squat, bench, deadlift, overhead press
- Long rest periods (3-5 min) between heavy sets
- Accessory work at moderate rep ranges

### Hypertrophy (Muscle Building)
- Primary: 8-12 reps, moderate-heavy loads
- 4-6 training days/week (split body parts)
- Include isolation work for target muscles
- Moderate rest (60-90s)
- Emphasize time under tension and mind-muscle connection

### Fat Loss
- Maintain strength training to preserve muscle
- Higher training density (shorter rest, supersets, circuits)
- Full-body or upper/lower splits to maximize calorie burn
- Include 2-3 conditioning sessions (HIIT, steady-state, or both)
- Note: diet is the primary driver; training supports

### General Fitness
- 3-4 training days/week, full-body or upper/lower
- Mix of rep ranges (strength + hypertrophy + endurance)
- Include some conditioning/cardio
- Prioritize movement variety and enjoyment
- Focus on functional movements

## Common Injury Accommodations

### Lower Back Pain
- Avoid heavy axial loading initially (back squats, conventional deadlifts)
- Substitute: goblet squats, leg press, trap bar deadlifts, hip thrusts
- Add core stability work (planks, bird dogs, dead bugs)
- Include hip mobility drills

### Shoulder Issues
- Avoid overhead pressing if painful; substitute landmine press or high incline
- Favor neutral grip for pressing and pulling
- Add rotator cuff prehab (band pull-aparts, face pulls, external rotations)
- Avoid behind-the-neck movements

### Knee Issues
- Limit deep squats if painful; use box squats or partial ROM
- Emphasize hip-dominant movements (hinges, hip thrusts)
- Strengthen VMO with terminal knee extensions
- Avoid high-impact plyometrics

### General Approach to Injuries
- Work around pain, not through it
- Always provide an alternative exercise for problematic movements
- Start with lighter loads and progress slowly
- Include prehab/rehab work for the affected area

## Warm-Up Protocol

Every session should begin with:
1. **General warm-up** (3-5 min): light cardio — rowing, cycling, brisk walking
2. **Dynamic mobility** (3-5 min): movements targeting the session's focus areas
   - Upper body day: arm circles, band pull-aparts, thoracic rotations
   - Lower body day: leg swings, hip circles, bodyweight squats, walking lunges
3. **Movement-specific warm-up**: 1-2 light sets of the first exercise

## Cooldown Protocol

Each session should end with:
1. **Light movement** (2-3 min): easy walk or cycling to bring heart rate down
2. **Static stretching** (3-5 min): hold stretches 20-30 seconds for worked muscles
3. Optional: foam rolling for tight areas

## Deload Weeks

- **Frequency**: every 4-6 weeks (every 3-4 for older or advanced trainees)
- **Method**: reduce volume by 40-50% OR reduce intensity by 10-15%
- **Purpose**: recovery, joint health, CNS reset
- Maintain movement patterns but at reduced stress
