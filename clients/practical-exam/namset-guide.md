## Trap Bar Deadlift

**N — Name** Trap Bar Deadlift

**A — Area** Lower body

**M — Muscles** 
- Glutes and hamstrings
- Quads, Erector spinae and traps

**S — Silent Demo** 
- Feet hip-width apart. 
- Hinge at the hips, push them back, grip the handles. 
- Chest tall, neutral spine, slight tension through the lats. 
- Drive through the floor, hips and shoulders rise together, 
- Lockout with glutes squeezed at the top. 
- Controlled descent back to the floor.

**E — Explanation** A powerful compound movement that trains the hip hinge pattern with less spinal stress than a barbell deadlift due to the centred load. You should feel the hamstrings load on the way down, and the glutes firing hard at lockout. It's both a strength and muscle builder for the entire posterior chain.

**T — Teaching**

- Step inside the bar, handles roughly in line with mid-foot
- Hinge to grip — don't squat down to it, push the hips back first
- Before pulling: big breath in, brace the core, "proud chest"
- Cue: _"Push the floor away"_ — this shifts focus onto leg drive rather than pulling with the back
- Exhale at the top of the rep
- Watch for: lower back rounding, hips shooting up before the shoulders, hyperextending at lockout
- For glute bias: focus on squeezing hard at the top and use a 2-sec hold before descending

## Goblet Squat

**N — Name** Goblet Squat

**A — Area** Lower body, bilateral

**M — Muscles** Quads are the primary driver. Glutes work through hip extension on the way up, particularly at depth. Core and upper back work isometrically to hold the weight and keep the torso upright.

**S — Silent Demo** Hold a dumbbell or kettlebell at chest height, elbows pointing down. Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out. Descend with an upright torso, elbows tracking inside the knees at the bottom. Full depth, pause briefly, drive back up through the heels.

**E — Explanation** The goblet squat teaches and reinforces good squat mechanics — the front-loaded weight acts as a counterbalance, making it easier to sit upright and reach full depth. You should feel the quads working throughout, and the glutes engaging as you drive out of the hole. A brief pause at the bottom eliminates momentum and increases time under tension.

**T — Teaching**

- Hold the weight close to the chest — don't let it drift forward
- Feet roughly shoulder-width, find a stance that lets you hit depth comfortably
- Inhale before descending, brace the core, exhale on the way up
- Cue: _"Spread the floor with your feet"_ — encourages knee tracking and glute activation
- Cue at the bottom: _"Tall chest, elbows inside the knees"_
- Watch for: forward torso lean, heels rising, knees caving inward

## One-arm Dumbbell Row

**N — Name** Single-Arm Dumbbell Row

**A — Area** Upper body, pull, unilateral

**M — Muscles** Latissimus dorsi is the primary mover, responsible for pulling the arm back and down. Rhomboids and mid-traps assist by retracting the scapula. Rear deltoid contributes at the top of the movement. Biceps act as a secondary puller throughout. Core and erectors work isometrically to maintain a stable, neutral spine.

**S — Silent Demo** Brace one hand and same-side knee on a bench, opposite foot on the floor. Torso parallel to the ground, neutral spine. Dumbbell hangs straight down from the shoulder. Pull the elbow back and up, driving it toward the hip, shoulder blade retracts at the top. Lower with control back to full hang.

**E — Explanation** A unilateral pulling movement that builds lat and upper back thickness while addressing side-to-side strength imbalances. You should feel a full stretch in the lat at the bottom and a strong contraction as the elbow passes the torso. The movement comes from the back — not the bicep.

**T — Teaching**

- Set up with a flat back — don't let the shoulder of the working arm drop and rotate open
- Let the dumbbell hang fully at the bottom to get the full stretch
- Inhale at the bottom, exhale as you row up
- Cue: _"Lead with the elbow, not the hand"_ — keeps the focus on the back
- Cue: _"Drive your elbow into your back pocket"_
- Watch for: shrugging the shoulder, rotating the torso to swing the weight up, cutting the range of motion short at the bottom

## Dead Bug

**N — Name** Dead Bug

**A — Area** Core, anterior chain

**M — Muscles** Transverse abdominis is the primary target — the deep stabilising muscle that controls intra-abdominal pressure and pelvic position. Rectus abdominis works to maintain spinal flexion throughout. Hip flexors are challenged as the legs lower and extend. Opposite arm and leg work simultaneously, demanding coordination and anti-rotation control.

**S — Silent Demo** Lie on your back, lower back pressed firmly into the floor. Arms straight up toward the ceiling, hips and knees at 90°. Lower one arm overhead and the opposite leg toward the floor simultaneously, keeping the lower back pinned. Return to start, repeat on the other side. No breath holding, no arching.

**E — Explanation** The dead bug trains the core to resist extension — meaning it stops your lower back from arching under load, which is exactly how the core functions during compound lifts. You should feel the deep abdominals working hard to keep your lower back flat. If the back lifts off the floor, the movement has broken down.

**T — Teaching**

- Before moving, tilt the pelvis to flatten the lower back into the floor — this position must be maintained throughout
- Move slowly and with full control — this is not a cardio exercise
- Inhale to prepare, exhale slowly as the arm and leg lower
- Cue: _"Push your lower back through the floor"_
- Cue: _"Move like you're trying not to spill something"_ — encourages control and stability
- Watch for: lower back arching off the floor, holding the breath, rushing the tempo, losing the 90° at the hip before the leg extends

## Bulgarian Split Squat

**N — Name** Bulgarian Split Squat

**A — Area** Lower body, unilateral (single leg)

**M — Muscles** Primarily quads and glutes. With a slight forward lean, the glute max becomes the dominant driver. Hamstrings assist at the hip, and the core works throughout to maintain stability.

**S — Silent Demo** Rear foot elevated on bench, front foot stepped well forward. Torso leans slightly forward, knee tracks over toes, hips drop straight down. No knee cave, pelvis stays level.

**E — Explanation** The goal is to load each leg independently, exposing and correcting side-to-side imbalances. You should feel a deep stretch in the rear hip flexor on the way down and a strong glute contraction driving you back up. The forward lean is intentional — it shifts load onto the glute rather than the quad.

**T — Teaching**

- Set up rear foot on the bench, laces down
- Step front foot far enough forward that your shin stays roughly vertical at the bottom
- Inhale at the top, descend with control, exhale as you drive up
- Cue: _"Drop your back knee straight down, don't let the front knee drift in"_
- Watch for: anterior pelvic tilt, torso shooting upright, rear knee flaring out

## Bench Press

**N — Name** Bench Press

**A — Area** Upper body, push

**M — Muscles** Pectorals are the primary mover, with the sternal (lower/outer) fibres doing the bulk of the work. Anterior deltoids assist at the shoulder, and the triceps drive the final extension at lockout. Scapular retractors work isometrically to create a stable base.

**S — Silent Demo** Lie flat, eyes under the bar. Feet flat on the floor, natural arch in the lower back, shoulder blades squeezed together and pressed into the bench. Unrack the bar, lower it with control to the lower chest, slight tuck of the elbows. Brief touch at the chest, press back up to lockout.

**E — Explanation** A fundamental upper body pushing movement that builds chest, shoulder, and tricep strength. You should feel the chest stretching at the bottom and contracting as you press. The arch and scapular retraction aren't optional — they protect the shoulder joint and create a stable platform to push from.

**T — Teaching**

- Set up with shoulder blades pulled back and down before unracking — this stays throughout
- Grip slightly wider than shoulder-width, wrists stacked over elbows
- Inhale at the top, lower with control, exhale as you press
- Cue: _"Bend the bar"_ — encourages lat engagement and elbow tuck
- Cue: _"Drive your back into the bench as you press"_
- Watch for: flared elbows, feet lifting, wrists collapsing back, bar bouncing off the chest
